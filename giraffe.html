Giraffe

Giraffes are the tallest mammals on Earth, easily recognized by their long necks, towering legs, and distinctive coat patterns of irregular patches. These unique patterns not only help with camouflage but are also unique to each giraffe, much like human fingerprints. Native to the savannas, grasslands, and open woodlands of Africa, giraffes primarily feed on the leaves, flowers, and fruits of tall trees, particularly acacias, using their long necks to reach vegetation inaccessible to other herbivores. Their long, prehensile tongues, which can extend up to 18 inches, are specially adapted to grasp and strip leaves from branches, even those protected by thorns. Despite their height and seemingly awkward structure, giraffes are remarkably graceful, capable of running at speeds up to 35 miles per hour over short distances. Giraffes are generally social animals, often forming loose, changing herds with other giraffes. While they are generally peaceful, they can use their powerful legs to deliver strong kicks when threatened. Unfortunately, giraffe populations are declining due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-wildlife conflict, making conservation efforts increasingly important to protect these gentle giants.
